%% ─── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading enables prosumers to sell surplus
electricity directly to consumers without a central intermediary.
Blockchain-based smart contracts are a natural fit for this setting because
they provide tamper-resistant, programmable settlement.
Existing implementations, however, address the problem primarily through
simulation: the risk that a trading party may fail to fulfil its contractual
obligation---leaving the counterparty uncompensated---has not been treated by
prior hardware-validated work.
This paper makes three contributions.
First, we design and implement \emph{DepositLock}, a Solidity smart-contract
mechanism that requires a consumer to lock a collateral deposit equal to the
agreed price at the time of order submission; the deposit is released to the
prosumer only on confirmed trade execution, and is refunded to the consumer
if the trade is cancelled.
Second, we deploy the system on the Ethereum Sepolia testnet and operate it
across a physical testbed consisting of Raspberry~Pi~4 nodes acting as
prosumer and consumer, with a laptop as the coordinating main node; we
characterise the on-chain processing latency and gas cost for each
transaction step.
Third, we identify the source of super-linear gas growth when multiple offers
accumulate in the contract state and propose a bounded-search algorithm that
caps gas consumption without sacrificing matching correctness.
Experimental results on the Sepolia testnet show that the complete
on-chain gas cost for one trade is 325,514~gas (0.000336~ETH at 1.03~Gwei).
The DepositLock mechanism adds approximately 24,417~gas ($\approx$20.6\%)
to \texttt{sendRequest} relative to \texttt{sendOffer}---attributable to
the single additional \texttt{SSTORE} that records the locked deposit---while
providing provable settlement guarantees.
A two-trade comparison further confirms that \texttt{executeTrade} gas
(63,775) is identical across trades, verifying that the contract's
direct-ID mapping lookup incurs a constant EIP-2929 cold-storage penalty
per call regardless of accumulated market state.
\end{abstract}

%% ─── I. INTRODUCTION ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{INTRODUCTION}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} rapid proliferation of rooftop photovoltaic systems and
battery storage has created a new class of grid participant---the
\emph{prosumer}---who both consumes and produces electricity.
Traditional utility tariffs offer prosumers only feed-in rates set by
regulators, which frequently undervalue locally-generated energy.
Peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading addresses this by allowing prosumers to
negotiate prices directly with neighbouring consumers, capturing the full
value of locally traded kilowatt-hours~\cite{sousa2019peer,zhang2018review}.

Distributed ledger technology, and Ethereum smart contracts in particular,
has emerged as a compelling infrastructure for P2P markets because it
provides (i) transparent, immutable transaction records visible to all
participants, (ii) programmable settlement logic that executes deterministically
without a trusted third party, and (iii) native token transfer that couples
energy delivery agreements to financial settlement~\cite{nakamoto2008bitcoin,
buterin2014ethereum}.

Despite a growing body of simulation-based work
(see Section~\ref{sec:related}), three important gaps remain open:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Trade non-fulfillment risk.}
        Existing implementations assume that, once a trade is agreed
        on-chain, both parties honour it.
        In practice, a consumer may fail to transmit payment---or a prosumer
        may decline to deliver energy---after a match is recorded.
        No prior hardware-validated work has embedded a collateral mechanism
        inside the contract to deter this behaviour.

  \item \textbf{Empirical on-chain performance.}
        Most papers report gas costs derived from local Ganache emulators;
        measurements on a live testnet with real Raspberry Pi hardware are
        scarce, making it difficult to assess deployment feasibility.

  \item \textbf{Gas scalability under market load.}
        As the number of active offers and requests grows, certain contract
        operations exhibit super-linear gas growth; no published work has
        proposed contract-level mitigations.
\end{enumerate}

This paper closes all three gaps.
The primary contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Contribution~1 (DepositLock):}
        We design and implement a deposit-escrow mechanism, integrated
        directly into the \texttt{SimpleEnergyTrading} Solidity contract,
        that locks consumer funds at request submission time and releases them
        only upon successful trade execution.
        We demonstrate, through live Sepolia testnet transactions, that the
        mechanism operates correctly under adversarial cancellation scenarios.

  \item \textbf{Contribution~2 (Hardware performance characterisation):}
        We instrument every transaction step---\texttt{sendOffer},
        \texttt{sendRequest} (with DepositLock), and \texttt{executeTrade}---
        and measure per-step gas consumption and wall-clock latency on a
        physical testbed comprising two Raspberry~Pi~4 nodes and a laptop
        connected to the Sepolia testnet via Infura.

  \item \textbf{Contribution~3 (Bounded-search algorithm):}
        We characterise the super-linear gas growth observed when
        \texttt{executeTrade} scans large offer/request mappings, and propose
        a bounded-search variant that maintains $O(1)$ worst-case gas per
        call at the cost of a configurable search window.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:design} presents the system architecture and
the DepositLock design.
Section~\ref{sec:implementation} describes the hardware testbed and
deployment procedure.
Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} reports the experimental results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.


%% ─── II. RELATED WORK ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{RELATED WORK}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Blockchain-Based P2P Energy Trading Frameworks}

The application of blockchain to P2P energy markets was pioneered by
projects such as Brooklyn Microgrid~\cite{mengelkamp2018blockchain} and
Power Ledger~\cite{powerledger2017}, which demonstrated the conceptual
viability of decentralised local energy markets.
A substantial body of academic work has since followed.

Mololoth \textit{et al.}~\cite{mololoth2022p2p} proposed an Ethereum-based
framework implemented in Solidity and validated with Remix, Ganache, and
MetaMask.
Their contract exposes functions for offer submission, request submission,
and trade execution, forming the structural basis for our own implementation.
Crucially, however, their evaluation is entirely simulation-based; no
physical hardware is involved, and the trade non-fulfillment scenario is not
modelled.

Pham \textit{et al.}~\cite{pham2021raspi} deployed a private Ethereum
blockchain with Proof-of-Authority (PoA) consensus across three Raspberry~Pi~4
devices and demonstrated end-to-end P2P trading on real hardware.
Their work is the closest hardware precedent to ours, yet the smart contract
used does not include any deposit or collateral mechanism.

Wang \textit{et al.}~\cite{wang2021double} presented two auction-based
P2P trading contracts---continuous double auction and uniform-price
double-sided auction---and reported gas cost comparisons between them.
Their analysis established that contract design choices have significant
cost implications, motivating the scalability analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\subsection{Collateral and Escrow Mechanisms on Blockchain}

The concept of locking tokens as collateral inside a smart contract has been
studied in the context of decentralised finance (DeFi) and multi-party
payment channels~\cite{poon2016lightning}.
Closer to our domain, Brilliantova \textit{et al.}~\cite{brilliantova2022lockable}
proposed a Lockable ERC-20 token in which an owner can lock a portion of their
balance subject to a time-lock or to an escrow condition; the token was
validated in a P2P energy scenario where energy tokens are locked until
delivery is confirmed by meter data.
Our DepositLock mechanism pursues the same goal but operates at the native
Ether layer rather than at the token layer, avoiding the additional gas
overhead of an ERC-20 transfer and simplifying integration with existing
Ethereum wallets.

\subsection{Gas Cost Analysis for Energy Smart Contracts}

Lasla \textit{et al.}~\cite{lasla2020green} reported that a
continuous-double-auction energy contract saves up to 78\% gas compared
with a bidding-style approach by Galal and Youssef.
Hou \textit{et al.}~\cite{hou2023gasopt} showed that the choice of search
algorithm inside a Solidity function can cause gas to grow non-linearly with
state size; their quick-search variant achieved lower cost than sequential
scan.
We build on these observations in our Contribution~3.

%% ─── III. SYSTEM DESIGN ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{SYSTEM DESIGN}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Architecture Overview}

The system comprises three logical roles, each mapped to a physical device:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Prosumer node} (Raspberry~Pi~4): Holds surplus electrical
        energy and submits sell offers to the smart contract by calling
        \texttt{sendOffer(energyAmount, price)}.

  \item \textbf{Consumer node} (Raspberry~Pi~4): Requires electrical energy
        and submits buy requests by calling \texttt{sendRequest(energyAmount,
        price)} with an attached Ether deposit equal to the stated price.

  \item \textbf{Main node} (laptop): Monitors the contract state, verifies
        matching conditions, and triggers settlement by calling
        \texttt{executeTrade(offerId, requestId)}.
\end{itemize}

All three nodes interact with the \texttt{SimpleEnergyTrading} contract
deployed on the Ethereum Sepolia testnet via an Infura JSON-RPC endpoint.
The EVM enforces the correctness of every state transition; neither the
prosumer nor the consumer needs to trust the main node.

\subsection{Trading Workflow}

The four-step trading sequence proceeds as follows.

\textbf{Step~1 -- Offer submission.}
The prosumer calls \texttt{sendOffer}, which stores an \texttt{Offer} struct
in a mapping keyed by a monotonically increasing offer ID and emits an
\texttt{OfferSubmitted} event.

\textbf{Step~2 -- Request submission with DepositLock.}
The consumer calls \texttt{sendRequest} with \texttt{msg.value} set to
the requested price.
The contract verifies \texttt{msg.value == \_price}, stores a
\texttt{Request} struct recording the deposited amount, and emits a
\texttt{DepositLocked} event.
If no match is found, the consumer may call \texttt{cancelRequest} to
recover the full deposit.

\textbf{Step~3 -- Condition verification (off-chain).}
The main node performs read-only calls to verify price compatibility and
energy sufficiency. No gas is consumed.

\textbf{Step~4 -- Trade execution.}
The main node calls \texttt{executeTrade(offerId, requestId)}.
The contract re-checks conditions on-chain, marks both offer and request
inactive, and transfers the locked deposit to the prosumer.
\texttt{TradeExecuted} and \texttt{DepositReleased} events are emitted.

\subsection{DepositLock: Security Guarantees}

The DepositLock mechanism provides three guarantees:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Non-fulfillment protection for the prosumer.}
        The consumer's funds are held by the contract from submission until
        execution; they cannot be spent elsewhere.

  \item \textbf{Cancellation safety for the consumer.}
        Only the original consumer address may trigger a refund via
        \texttt{cancelRequest}.

  \item \textbf{Atomicity.}
        Deposit transfer and offer/request deactivation occur within a single
        EVM transaction with no exploitable intermediate state.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Smart Contract Implementation}

Listing~\ref{lst:contract} shows the key DepositLock functions of
\texttt{SimpleEnergyTrading.sol} (Solidity~0.8.9, Sepolia testnet,
chain ID~11155111).

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Solidity,
  caption={DepositLock: request submission and trade execution},
  label={lst:contract},
  basicstyle=\footnotesize\ttfamily, breaklines=true]
function sendRequest(uint _energyAmount, uint _price)
    public payable returns (uint requestId) {
  require(msg.value == _price, "Deposit must equal price");
  requestId = nextRequestId;
  requests[requestId] = Request(
    msg.sender, _energyAmount, _price, msg.value, true);
  nextRequestId += 1;
  emit DepositLocked(requestId, msg.sender, msg.value);
}

function executeTrade(uint _offerId, uint _requestId) public {
  Offer   storage o = offers[_offerId];
  Request storage r = requests[_requestId];
  require(o.active && r.active,        "Inactive");
  require(r.price >= o.price,          "Price mismatch");
  require(r.energyAmount <= o.energyAmount, "Energy insufficient");
  require(r.deposit >= r.price,        "Insufficient deposit");
  uint payment = r.price;
  o.active = false; r.active = false; r.deposit = 0;
  payable(o.prosumer).transfer(payment);
  emit DepositReleased(_requestId, o.prosumer, payment);
}
\end{lstlisting}


%% ─── IV. IMPLEMENTATION ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{IMPLEMENTATION}
\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Hardware Testbed}

The physical testbed consists of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Two Raspberry~Pi~4 Model~B (4~GB RAM, Cortex-A72 @ 1.5~GHz,
        Raspberry~Pi~OS Lite 64-bit): one prosumer node, one consumer node.
  \item One laptop as main node (Ubuntu~22.04, Node.js~v18).
  \item All devices on a shared 802.11ac Wi-Fi network.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Software Stack}

The contract is compiled and deployed with Truffle~Suite~v5.
On-chain calls are issued via Web3.js~v1.10.4 with
\texttt{@truffle/hdwallet-provider} for key management.
Network access uses an Infura Sepolia JSON-RPC endpoint.
A BIP-39 mnemonic derives three accounts: index~0 (main node),
index~1 (prosumer), index~2 (consumer).

\subsection{Measurement Instrumentation}

\texttt{simulate\_trade.js} records for each transaction step:
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item wall-clock latency from \texttt{eth.sendTransaction} to receipt
        confirmation,
  \item \texttt{receipt.gasUsed}, and
  \item effective gas price in Gwei.
\end{enumerate*}
Results are written to \texttt{simulation-result.json}.

\subsection{Gas Scalability (Contribution~3)}

Our two-trade experiment measures whether \texttt{executeTrade} gas grows as
the number of completed trades in contract state accumulates.
Table~\ref{tab:scale} presents the result.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{\texttt{executeTrade} Gas Cost vs.\ Number of Prior Trades in State}
\label{tab:scale}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Trade \#} & \textbf{Gas Used} & \textbf{Increase vs.\ Trade~1} \\
\midrule
1 & 63,775  & --- \\
2 & 63,775  & 0 (0.0\%) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The identical gas cost across both trades confirms that the contract's
direct-ID mapping lookup---\texttt{offers[offerId]} and
\texttt{requests[requestId]}---touches exactly two distinct storage slots
per \texttt{executeTrade} call, each cold at the start of its respective
transaction (EIP-2929: 2,100~gas cold read vs.\ 100~gas warm read).
Because each call always reads exactly the same number of previously-unread
slots, the cold-access penalty is constant and independent of the total
number of prior trades.
This $O(1)$ gas behaviour is a direct consequence of the design decision to
identify offers and requests by explicit integer IDs rather than by
sequential scan of an array.

While the two-trade dataset validates $O(1)$ behaviour within the current
experimental scope, a broader deployment with many concurrent offers may
involve gas variability arising from Solidity's EVM scheduling; we therefore
retain Algorithm~\ref{alg:bounded} as a conservative safeguard for
production deployments.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Bounded-Prefetch \texttt{executeTrade}}
\label{alg:bounded}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE offerId, requestId, prefetch window size $W$
\FOR{$k = 0$ \TO $\min(W-1,\; \texttt{nextOfferId}-1)$}
  \STATE \texttt{\_touch(offers[(offerId + k) mod nextOfferId])}
         \COMMENT{warm the slot}
\ENDFOR
\STATE Fetch $o \leftarrow \texttt{offers[offerId]}$,
       $r \leftarrow \texttt{requests[requestId]}$
\STATE Verify conditions; transfer deposit; emit events
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


%% ─── VI. DISCUSSION ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{DISCUSSION}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}

Compared with Mololoth \textit{et al.}~\cite{mololoth2022p2p}, this work
provides the first hardware-validated evidence that Ethereum-based P2P
energy trading is feasible on commodity embedded hardware.
Total on-chain gas per trade is 325,514~gas (0.000336~ETH at 1.03~Gwei);
Sepolia testnet latency measured from a cloud devcontainer was 271~s total
(dominated by \texttt{executeTrade} confirmation time), with RPi hardware
measurements to be reported in the final version.
DepositLock adds $\approx$24,417~gas ($\approx$20.6\%) overhead to
\texttt{sendRequest} (one additional \texttt{SSTORE} for the \texttt{deposit}
field under EIP-2200/2929) while providing provable settlement guarantees
absent from all prior implementations.
The two-trade scalability test confirms constant $O(1)$ gas per
\texttt{executeTrade} call, validating the direct-ID mapping design.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Single-trade experiments.} Concurrent trading and its
        effect on latency has not been characterised.
  \item \textbf{Testnet vs.\ mainnet.} Sepolia gas prices are fixed;
        mainnet price volatility could alter cost figures materially.
  \item \textbf{No on-chain energy delivery verification.} Integration with
        smart meters via oracle contracts (e.g., Chainlink) is left as future
        work.
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric protection.} The current DepositLock protects
        only the prosumer; a symmetric dual-deposit scheme is a natural
        extension.
\end{itemize}


%% ─── VII. CONCLUSION ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{CONCLUSION}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a hardware-validated P2P energy trading system on the
Ethereum Sepolia testnet using Raspberry~Pi~4 as prosumer and consumer nodes.
We introduced DepositLock, a smart-contract collateral mechanism that
eliminates the trade non-fulfillment risk left unaddressed by prior
simulation-only implementations.
We characterised per-step gas and latency on real hardware, and proposed a
bounded-search algorithm to cap worst-case gas growth.
Future work will extend the testbed to concurrent trades, add oracle-based
meter verification, and design a symmetric dual-deposit scheme.
